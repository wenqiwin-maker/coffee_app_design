Overview

The Challenge

Choosing coffee feels like a gamble. Users scroll reviews, guess at flavor notes like "Rich & Bold," and often waste $18–30 on beans they don't like.

Discovery is fragmented across 5–7 websites, and abstract descriptions don't help users understand what they'll actually taste.

70% want personalized coffee recommendations
45% buy coffee that doesn't match their real taste
5-7 websites browsed before purchasing
200% subscription market growth in 5 years

Research

Understanding the User

Through 4 rounds of user research with everyday home brewers (ages 25–40), I uncovered key friction points in coffee discovery.

!
Expression Gap

Words like "smooth" and "bright" don't help users imagine the real taste.

!
Fear of Waste

One wrong bag means 1–2 weeks of disappointing mornings.

!
Scattered Discovery

Users jump between Amazon, YouTube, Reddit, and cafés with no unified flow.

Solution

BeanQuest: Discover Coffee with Confidence

A personalized discovery platform that combines AI-powered taste matching, transparent recommendations, and low-risk sampling.

AI Compare

Side-by-side comparisons with "Why It Wins" reasoning based on your taste profile.

Taste Matching

Behavior-based preference learning that adapts to what you actually like.

Sample First

Try before you commit with curated sample kits aligned to AI recommendations.

Design

From Concept to Prototype

Two rounds of prototyping and testing refined the experience.

V1 → V2 Key Changes

  • Added "Why It Wins" transparency for AI recommendations
  • Quick Actions for faster exploration paths
  • Multi-source insights (barista notes, reviews, price history)
  • Clearer IA separating "For You" vs "Explore More"

Results

Impact & Learnings

Usability testing with 5 users validated the core experience and revealed significant behavioral shifts.

100%

reported higher decision clarity with "Why It Wins"

80%

3x

SUS scores across all tasks

Key Takeaways

Trust is foundational

AI must explain, not just recommend

Reduce risk to increase exploration

Sampling converted hesitant users into confident explorers

Behavior over questionnaires

Preference learning works better through actions than stated preferences
